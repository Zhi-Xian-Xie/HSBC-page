Robust Reward Alignment via Hypothesis Space Batch Cutting

Zhixian Xie*1  , Haode Zhang*2  , Yizhe Feng2  , Wanxin Jin1
1Arizona State University 2Shanghai Jiao Tong University

Abstract

Reward design in reinforcement learning is challenging, especially when human preferences contain errors. We propose a robust and efficient alignment method called Hypothesis Space Batch Cutting (HSBC), which refines the reward space by making conservative "cuts" based on batch-wise human feedback. By grouping preferences and applying disagreement-based queries, HSBC improves robustness against false preferences without needing to explicitly identify them.

Our method is evaluated across diverse control tasks and achieves competitive results under clean settings, while significantly outperforming baselines under high-noise conditions.

Core Method Overview

HSBC iteratively refines the reward hypothesis space through three key steps:

HSBC Algorithm:
1. Hypothesis Sampling: Sample reward parameters from the current hypothesis space
2. Disagreement-based Query: Select trajectory pairs where model ensemble disagrees most
3. Hypothesis Space Cutting: Update the hypothesis space using batched human preferences

Below we illustrate key components of the HSBC algorithm. It starts by selecting trajectory pairs from regions where ensemble policies disagree most, then progressively cuts the hypothesis space based on human feedback.

With clean preferences, the cuts accurately isolate the true reward region as shown in Fig. 1. However, false or noisy preferences can cause naive cuts that incorrectly eliminate the correct hypotheses.

Fig. 3 Left: Naive cut caused by false preference erroneously removes true reward hypotheses.
Fig. 3 Right: HSBC’s robust, conservative cut preserves the true reward region despite false feedback.

To handle noisy human preferences, HSBC applies a conservative cutting threshold that tolerates some false feedback, thus preserving the true reward region and enabling reliable learning.

Results

We evaluate HSBC on a diverse set of reinforcement learning and model predictive control tasks, including locomotion (Cartpole, Walker, Humanoid, Go2-Standup) and dexterous manipulation (Dexman-Cube, Dexman-Bunny).

Our method achieves performance comparable to or better than state-of-the-art baselines such as PREF-BI and Disagreement Learning in noise-free settings. Under high levels of incorrect human feedback (up to 30%), HSBC significantly outperforms existing approaches in both reward accuracy and task performance.

These results demonstrate HSBC's strong robustness and sample efficiency in aligning with true reward functions, even in the presence of substantial label noise.
